<p>A payment gateway moves rupees once. It authorizes a card or a UPI request, settles the money, and fires a success webhook. That is where most integration guides stop, and that is exactly where the real work of a subscription business begins. Recurring revenue is not one payment repeated. It is a relationship you have to manage on a schedule, forever, and no gateway sells you that layer.</p> <p>In India the gap is wider than the Stripe-world tutorials admit. You cannot just save a card and re-charge it whenever you like. You register a <strong>mandate</strong> the customer's bank approves, you live inside RBI's e-mandate rules, you respect the UPI Autopay per-debit cap, and you stamp a GST-correct tax invoice on every renewal. Get any one of those wrong and the debit fails or the invoice does not match the money in your account.</p> <p>This guide is the full build, in the order we actually build it: what a billing system is versus a gateway, the India mandate problem, the data model, the subscription state machine, proration maths, dunning, GST-compliant recurring invoices, the self-serve portal, revenue analytics, and an honest build-vs-buy call. We write it from production, not a vendor deck. Where a number depends on the current rulebook or your own pricing, we say so in the text, and you should verify it against your own setup before you ship.</p> <h2> What a subscription billing system actually is (and what a payment gateway is not) </h2> <p>A <strong>payment gateway collects money once</strong>. It authorizes a card, a UPI request, or a mandate, moves the rupees, and hands you a success webhook. That is the rail, and we covered how to pick it in our <a href="https://dev.to/blog/accept-payments-online-india-2026-upi-cards-autopay-costs/">accept payments in India</a> post, so we will not repeat it here.</p> <p>A <strong>billing system manages the relationship after the money moves</strong>, on repeat, forever. It is the layer no gateway sells you. Seven jobs live here:</p> <ul> <li> <strong>Plans and pricing:</strong> tiers, trials, coupons, metered usage</li> <li> <strong>Subscription lifecycle:</strong> activate, upgrade, pause, cancel</li> <li> <strong>Mandates:</strong> UPI Autopay, e-mandate, card-on-file recurring</li> <li> <strong>Proration:</strong> mid-cycle plan changes, credits, refunds</li> <li> <strong>Dunning:</strong> retry failed charges, notify, then suspend</li> <li> <strong>Recurring GST invoices:</strong> correct place of supply, HSN/SAC, IRN</li> <li> <strong>MRR and churn reporting:</strong> what is actually recurring, and what is leaking</li> </ul> <p><strong>Who has outgrown a raw gateway integration:</strong> Indian SaaS billing monthly, OTT and streaming, edtech, gym and coworking memberships, and D2C subscription boxes. The morning one card fails and nobody retries it, you have outgrown the gateway.</p> <p>We write this from production, not a vendor doc. We run recurring billing inside <a href="https://dev.to/products/plugev/">PlugEV</a> (metered EV session billing), <a href="https://dev.to/products/cloudnx/">CloudNX</a> (INR hosting renewals), and <a href="https://dev.to/products/crm-multivendor/">MultiVendor CRM</a> (per-tenant plans).</p> <p>The thesis is simple: <strong>you will build this layer whether you design it on purpose or it grows by accident.</strong> The accidental version still runs. It just leaks money quietly.</p> <h2> The India mandate problem: UPI Autopay, e-NACH, and RBI e-mandate rules </h2> <p>In India, a recurring charge is not a saved card you re-charge whenever you like. You first register a <strong>mandate</strong>, a standing instruction the customer's bank approves, and every future debit runs against that mandate's limits and rules. Get the mandate wrong and the debit simply fails, no matter how clean your billing logic is.</p> <p><strong>UPI Autopay</strong> is the easiest to onboard, but it carries an AFA-free threshold of <strong>Rs 15,000</strong> per debit. A debit at or below Rs 15,000 auto-debits hands-free; a debit above Rs 15,000 is allowed, but it triggers additional factor authentication (an OTP) on every single debit, which defeats the point of hands-free billing. That single number shapes your architecture. A Rs 24,000 annual plan cannot ride UPI Autopay hands-free, because a single debit above Rs 15,000 triggers an OTP every cycle. You either route high-value plans to another mandate type or split the amount into smaller debits that stay under the threshold. An enhanced Rs 1,00,000 AFA-free limit does exist, but only for specific categories (insurance premiums, mutual funds, and credit-card bills), not general SaaS.</p> <p><strong>e-NACH / e-mandate</strong> covers higher-value collections and bank-account debits, with per-mandate limits you set at registration. These are not capped at Rs 1,00,000: that figure is the enhanced AFA-free category limit, not a universal e-NACH ceiling. General e-NACH per-mandate limits can run much higher, and banks support well above Rs 1,00,000 (the exact ceiling varies by sponsor bank and gateway). <strong>Card e-mandates</strong> are the third rail for card-first customers. Your system should pick the mandate type per plan, not force one rail on everyone.</p> <p><strong>RBI e-mandate rules that become code.</strong> Two things you must build in: a <strong>pre-debit notification</strong> sent to the customer at least 24 hours before each charge, and <strong>additional factor of authentication</strong> on the first registration (and on debits above the AFA threshold). You also cannot store the raw card number. You work with <strong>network tokens</strong> only, the same tokenisation flow we cover in our accept-payments guide.</p> <p><strong>Model the mandate as a first-class object.</strong> Give it its own row and lifecycle: <code>created</code>, <code>active</code>, <code>paused</code>, <code>revoked</code>, <code>expired</code>. Do not bury the mandate as a hidden field on the subscription, because a paused or revoked mandate can outlive or precede a plan change, and you need that history when a debit bounces.</p> <p>Every debit and every mandate event flows back through webhooks. Apply the same idempotent, callback-driven discipline we detail in our Razorpay webhooks post: one handler, keyed on event ID, safe to replay.</p> <h2> The data model that makes or breaks it </h2> <p>Get the schema wrong and every feature you bolt on later fights you. We have rebuilt billing tables for clients who baked prices into plans, and the migration is never a weekend job. Here is the shape that holds up.</p> <p><strong>Split product, plan, and price into three tables.</strong> A <code>product</code> is the thing you sell (your Pro tier). A <code>plan</code> is a billing shape (monthly, annual). A <code>price</code> is the actual amount. One plan carries many prices: monthly INR, annual INR, USD for overseas users, a Diwali promo. Never store the amount on the plan row. The day you launch annual billing, you will thank yourself.</p> <p><strong>Model subscriptions with subscription_items.</strong> A <code>subscription</code> links a customer to a plan, but the real charges live in <code>subscription_items</code>. That is how one customer holds a base seat, three extra seats, and an SMS add-on without you inventing a flat combined charge. Proration later reads from these items.</p> <p><strong>Invoices are append-only.</strong> Keep <code>invoices</code>, <code>invoice_line_items</code>, and <code>credit_notes</code> as separate records. Once an invoice is finalized you never edit it. To correct it you issue a <strong>credit note</strong>. This is not a preference, it is a GST requirement: a finalized tax invoice is an immutable document, and adjustments happen through credit notes with their own numbering series.</p> <p><strong>Mandates and payment_attempts get their own tables.</strong> An e-mandate (UPI Autopay, card, or eNACH under the RBI framework) has a lifecycle. Every debit, retry, and failure needs a row in <code>payment_attempts</code> so dunning has a real audit trail, not a status field you overwrite.</p> <p><strong>Money and immutability.</strong> Store amounts as <strong>integer paise</strong>, never floating point. Never hard-delete a billing row; soft-delete or void.</p> <p><strong>Tenant isolation from day one.</strong> In a multi-tenant SaaS, scope every billing table by <code>tenant_id</code>. See our <a href="https://dev.to/blog/multi-tenant-saas-architecture-guide-india-2026/#billing">per-tenant billing section</a> for the isolation model.</p> <h2> The subscription lifecycle as a state machine </h2> <p>Every billing bug we have cleaned up traces back to a subscription being in a state nobody named. So name them first.</p> <p><strong>The six states worth modeling:</strong> <code>trialing</code>, <code>active</code>, <code>past_due</code>, <code>paused</code>, <code>canceled</code>, and <code>expired</code>. Fix the legal transitions and forbid the rest. <code>trialing</code> goes to <code>active</code> or <code>canceled</code>, never straight to <code>expired</code>. <code>active</code> slips to <code>past_due</code> when a debit fails, then either recovers to <code>active</code> or ends in <code>canceled</code> after dunning gives up. <code>paused</code> only comes from <code>active</code> and only returns to <code>active</code>.</p> <p><strong>Drive transitions from events, not a cron job guessing.</strong> A mandate debit succeeded, a debit failed, a cancel was requested: each is an event that moves the machine. Razorpay tells you this through webhooks (<code>subscription.charged</code>, <code>subscription.halted</code>), so handle them idempotently. Store the event ID, check it before you act, and a retried webhook never double-advances a subscription. See our <a href="https://dev.to/blog/razorpay-webhooks-idempotency-reconciliation-india-2026/">Razorpay webhooks guide</a> for the dedupe pattern.</p> <p><strong>Trials and coupons touch the invoice, not the mandate.</strong> A 14-day trial or a first-cycle 30% off changes what the first invoice charges. The e-mandate you registered still authorizes the full recurring amount (though on UPI Autopay any debit above ₹15,000 needs an OTP each time, so it is not truly hands-free above that threshold). Discount the line item, keep the mandate intact.</p> <p><strong>Pause and resume is a real Indian retention lever.</strong> Gym, OTT, and coworking members freeze for a month instead of canceling. A <code>paused</code> state that stops billing but keeps the mandate alive saves the relationship. Cancel does not.</p> <p><strong>The renewal engine</strong> is a scheduled job that finds due subscriptions, raises the GST invoice, and triggers the debit. Make it re-runnable: guard on <code>invoice_already_raised_for_period</code> so a re-run never bills twice.</p> <p><strong>Cancellation:</strong> <code>cancel_at_period_end</code> lets access run out the paid cycle; immediate cancel revokes now. Either way, cancel the mandate at the gateway so no orphan debit fires later.</p> <h2> Proration: the maths of mid-cycle upgrades and downgrades </h2> <p><strong>Proration in one line:</strong> when a customer changes plans mid-cycle, charge them fairly for the part of the cycle they actually used.</p> <p><strong>Upgrade path.</strong> Credit the unused time on the old plan, charge the new plan immediately, and net the difference. The customer pays the gap now and their renewal date usually stays the same.</p> <p><strong>Downgrade path.</strong> Do not refund cash. Bank a <strong>credit</strong> and apply it against the next invoice. Refunds trigger reconciliation, RBI mandate mess, and support tickets you do not want.</p> <p><strong>Compute it without bugs.</strong> Derive a <strong>per-second or per-day rate</strong> from the full cycle, not a rounded monthly figure. Work in <strong>integer paise</strong> end to end and round once, at the very end. Example: a customer on the ₹999 plan upgrades to ₹2,499 on day 20 of a 30-day cycle. Unused credit is 999 × (10/30) = ₹333. New plan charge for the remaining 10 days is 2,499 × (10/30) = ₹833. They pay ₹500 today.</p> <p><strong>Proration is invoicing, not just arithmetic.</strong> A downgrade credit becomes a <strong>GST credit note</strong> against the original invoice. An upgrade charge is a <strong>new taxable line</strong> at 18% GST. Get the document type wrong and your GSTR filing breaks.</p> <p><strong>The harder cousin: metered billing.</strong> On <strong>PlugEV</strong> the charge depends on kWh consumed, not a flat plan, so we rate usage events into paise continuously and invoice the total at cycle close. Same integer discipline, more moving parts.</p> <p><strong>We test the proration engine like a payments path.</strong> An off-by-one-day error looks tiny in a unit test and bleeds real money at scale, so we run fixed-clock cases across every upgrade, downgrade, and trial-conversion boundary.</p> <h2> Dunning: how to stop involuntary churn eating your MRR </h2> <p>Not all churn is a customer walking away. <strong>Voluntary churn</strong> is someone who cancels on purpose. <strong>Involuntary churn</strong> is someone who wanted to stay but whose auto-debit failed. Industry estimates put involuntary churn (failed payments and expired mandates) at roughly 20 to 40 percent of total subscription churn, and that slice is recoverable if you build for it.</p> <p><strong>The retry ladder.</strong> Don't hammer a failed mandate the same day. Space retries out, for example day 1, 3, 5, and 7, and tune the schedule to the actual failure reason. In India the common ones are insufficient balance, a paused mandate, and bank downtime. Insufficient balance clears near salary dates, so a day-5 or day-7 retry often lands when day-1 won't. Razorpay Subscriptions runs a retry schedule but gives you limited control over its timing and messaging; a rules-based cadence like this, with your own retry days and pre-debit copy, needs a dedicated tool like Chargebee or a custom dunning layer.</p> <p><strong>Pre-debit notifications feed dunning.</strong> The RBI-mandated pre-debit heads-up (24 hours before the debit) is also your best churn-reducer, because customers top up their account when they see it coming. Treat it as the first rung of dunning, not a compliance checkbox.</p> <p><strong>Multi-channel, but respect the rules.</strong> Send reminders in-app, over email, and via WhatsApp or SMS. SMS and promotional WhatsApp go through DLT-registered templates, so wire your dunning copy into the same DLT-compliant notification pipeline you already use for transactional messages, or they won't deliver.</p> <p><strong>Grace period and the cancel path.</strong> Decide how long a subscription stays <code>past_due</code> before it expires. A 7 to 14 day grace window is common. Keep the customer's access live during grace so a recovered payment restores service without a break, then downgrade or expire once retries are exhausted.</p> <p><strong>Measure it.</strong> Track <strong>recovery rate</strong> (failed payments recovered divided by total failed) per cohort and per retry day. Without that number, dunning is hope. With it, you can move retry days and reminder channels and see the MRR you saved.</p> <h2> GST-compliant recurring invoices </h2> <p><strong>Invoice on the settled amount, not the attempt.</strong> When Razorpay or a NACH mandate fires a renewal, wait for the webhook that confirms the debit cleared, then generate the invoice against that captured payment. If you invoice on the gross plan price at the attempt stage, a failed or partial debit leaves you with an invoice that never matches the money in your account. Tie every invoice to a real <code>payment_id</code>, not a scheduled charge.</p> <p><strong>Keep the number series audit-clean.</strong> Invoice numbers must run sequentially per financial year and per GSTIN. It automatically shards data and handles failover for individual shards.</li> </ul> <p>Furthermore, Redis offers persistence options (<code>RDB</code> snapshots and <code>AOF</code> append-only file) to prevent data loss in case of a complete system restart, ensuring that even ephemeral session data can survive restarts if configured correctly.</p> <h2> Considerations for Production Deployments </h2> <ul> <li> <strong>Load Balancing:</strong> Use a robust load balancer (e.g., Nginx, HAProxy, AWS ALB) configured for sticky sessions (if session affinity is desired, though a stateless design with Redis reduces this need) and WebSocket proxying.</li> <li> <strong>Authentication &amp; Authorization:</strong> Integrate with your existing auth system. Sanitize all incoming client messages.</li> <li> <strong>Backpressure:</strong> Implement mechanisms to handle situations where consumers (clients or servers) cannot process messages as fast as they are produced, preventing memory exhaustion.</li> <li> <strong>Client-side Reconnection Logic:</strong> Clients must be designed to gracefully handle disconnections and attempt intelligent reconnections, potentially with exponential backoff.</li> </ul> <h2> Conclusion </h2> <p>Building resilient real-time systems requires careful consideration of scalability and fault tolerance beyond a single server. By coupling WebSockets for efficient communication with Redis for distributed state management and message brokering, engineers can construct highly available, scalable, and robust real-time applications. Redis's Pub/Sub and key-value capabilities make it an indispensable tool for ensuring that messages reach their intended recipients across a cluster of WebSocket servers and that user sessions can seamlessly persist through server failures, providing a foundation for truly resilient interactive experiences.</p>
